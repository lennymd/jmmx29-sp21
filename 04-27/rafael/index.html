Wealth and Prison

Rafael de la Zerda

When you’re jailed, money can make all the difference. In the United States, income has a significant impact on the experience of incarceration at every stage, interplaying with race and gender. The county’s infamously large prison population is largely due to its excessive bail, which restricts the principle of innocent until proven guilty to the wealthy. Moreover, those in lower income brackets are more likely to be jailed to begin with, and most of those unable to meet bail fall within the poorest third of society. A greater percentage of men than women unable to make bail are moderately wealthy, while women unable to make bail are evenly distributed across all economic brackets. The likelihood of incarceration is higher for blacks than for whites and Hispanics at every level of wealth. Racial wealth gaps have been observed among the future-incarcerated and the previously-incarcerated. The rate of unemployment is higher for those released from prison than for the general population. The rates are highest for black women and lowest for white men.

Prison Income Distributions by Gender

Monthly income categories for jailed individuals ages 23-39 prior to incarceration, for the incarcerated unable to meet bail, and for the non-incarcerated. Amounts are expressed in 2002 dollars. Men unable to make bail skewed toward higher salaries, likely due to higher overall income; women unable to make bail were more evenly distributed across categories. Though most jailed individuals make less than the non-incarcerated in their income bracket, there is a notable exception: more women making $0 were never incarcerated than those who were. One explanation is that many non-incarcerated women relied on their spouses.

Income of Incarcerated
(Pre-Incarceration) and
Never Incarcerated

Median annual incomes, prior to incarceration, for those aged 23-39 unable to meet bail in local jails, expressed in 2015 dollars. The median bail bond is nearly equivalent to a full year’s income for those unable to meet bail, on a national level. The median pre-incarceration income was $15,109, less than half the median for those not in jail of similar ages.

Unemployment Rates after Release

Unemployment rates of the formerly incarcerated compared to the general population, both ages 35-44. Results are broken down by age and gender. Rates were highest among blacks between both genders, in women between both races, and in the fomerly incarcer- ated across all categories.

Wealth Distribution of Previously
and Never Incarcerated

Annual income distribution of the previously and never incarcerated across the 4 quartiles of wealth, broken down by race. Data are from the National Longitudinal Survey of Youth, recorded across three nonconsecutive years. Greater wealth observed lower incarceration rates. At every economic level, each race had higher incomes if they had never been incarcerated. Whites had the greatest wealth in every category, blacks generally the least.
