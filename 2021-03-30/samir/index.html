What makes you more likely
to get a ticket?

There are millions of traffic tickets issued every year in the United States, largely to ensure lawfulness on the road. Without the threat of a large fee or a court order, there would be many drivers who would drive recklessly and at the expense of others. This is the benefit of traffic tickets; they make people more prone to driving carefully and attentively. There are many reasons by which a police officer may issue someone a ticket, and thus there are patterns associated with the issuance of tickets (i.e., by color of car, race, gender, age, etc.). This graphic is intended to illustrate and explore these patterns.

What color car gets ticketed the most?

The illustrations below take a look at the colors of cars that get ticketed the most frequently relative to the
number of these color cars on the road.

While they only constitute 6% of cars of total, solid gray cars account for 10% of tickets. In other words, for every solid gray car on the road, about 1.67 of them get a ticket!

While the stigma is that red cars get pulled over the most, the data says otherwise. They make up about 14% of cars on the road, yet account for 16% of tickets. This is a ratio of about 1.142.

White cars account for most of the total cars on the road at about 25%. However, they receive only 19% of tickets. This is a ratio of about 0.76 tickets per white car on the road.

Making up about 10% of cars on the road, silver cars account for 5% of tickets. This means that for every silver car on the road, only half of them tend to get a ticket.
