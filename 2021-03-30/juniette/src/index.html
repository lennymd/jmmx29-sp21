An Introduction to Belly Dance

Belly dance, a type of Middle Eastern dance, is an expressive and often improvisational dance that has historically been danced by women for other women. The true origin of the dance is unknown, but theories say that it may have originated from ritual dances or to help prepare the body for child birth. It is usually performed barefoot and with colorful costumes. Its popularity continues to grow worldwide.

Belly Dance Styles & Rhythms

Belly dance has many different styles that come from different areas of Egypt and other Middle Eastern countries. These are six common styles and their rhythms.

Raqs Sharqi

The modern form of Middle Eastern dance, also known as oriential dance. This style is more familiar in the west and is performed in restaurants and theaters around the world. It uses a two-piece costume and often has a dramatic entrance where the dancers enter with a veil.
Rhythms: Masmoudi Kebir, Masmoudi Zghire, Maqsoum

Baladi

The main focus of this social dance is on the hips, with little use of arms. The typical costume covers the midriff and is most commonly a straight skirt with side slits and long sleeves. A sash and headscarf is often worn.
Rhythms: Masmoudi Zghire

Saidi

A folkloric dance originating in the Southern region of Egypt known as “El Said” (the countryside). It is inspired by Tahtib, the oldest form of Egyptian martial arts, which used long sticks as weapons. Today, women perform their own version of this dance with canes as a way of playfully imitating the men.
Rhythms: Saidi

Khaleeji

A style of dance from the Persian Gulf. Dancers wear oversized dresses, with the sleeves held up lirtatiously. The dance uses footwork patterns that give a gliding feeling. It also incorporates hair tosses, head slides, and shoulder movements.
Rhythms: Khaleeji

Zaar

A ritualistic and ancient practice where one cleanses the body of evil spirits. It is believed to have originated in Ethiopia. It has an earthy and heavy feel, dancers simulate the Zar by using the chest and head movements.
Rhythms: Ayoub

Zeffa

Traditionally, a zeffa is a joyous wedding procession in Egypt and other Middle Eastern countries. The dancer balances a shamadan (candelabra) on her head and plays zills (finger cymbals).
Rhythms: Zeffa, Chiftetelli
